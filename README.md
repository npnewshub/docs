# NP News Hub — Documentation

**NP News Hub** is a **news intelligence system** for Nepali news. It is a **public-facing project**: anyone can read this document to understand what the project is, why it exists, what advantages it offers, and what positive impact it aims to bring for the general public, the media, and society in Nepal.

This document explains the project in detail — what it does, how it helps, and why it matters.

---

## What is NP News Hub?

NP News Hub is a system that:

1. **Collects and indexes news** from selected Nepali news sources. Articles are stored and processed so that their *meaning* (not just keywords) can be searched.
2. **Lets you ask questions in Nepali or English** and returns answers that are **only** from those indexed articles. Every answer is tied to **source links** — the exact headlines and URLs — so you can see where the information came from and read the full story yourself.
3. **Tracks which outlet published what and when**, so over time the system can show who broke a story first and who republished it. Each publisher (“house”) can have its own **report**: what they published, how they framed the news, and whether their past claims were later verified.
4. **Uses a knowledge-graph design** so that in the future it can represent *who* said *what*, *where*, and *when* — and whether those claims held up. That supports transparency, accountability, and evidence-based public discourse.

So NP News Hub is both a **working tool today** (search and Q&A over real news) and a **platform** for greater transparency and accountability as more features are built. It is designed to serve the **general public in Nepal** first: citizens, readers, and anyone who wants to find, verify, and understand news without relying on a single outlet or on unverified social media.

## How does this system help the general public?

### You get answers that you can verify

When you ask a question, the system does **not** search the entire internet or mix in unverified content. It searches **only** the news articles that have been indexed in the project. So every answer is **grounded in real, published journalism**. You always get **source links** — the names of the outlets and the URLs of the articles — so you can click through and read the original. That helps you form your own opinion based on evidence, not on rumor or opaque algorithms.

### You can search by meaning, in your own language

Many people think in ideas — for example, “election preparation in Parsa” or “what are parties saying about the budget?” — not in exact headlines. **Semantic search** understands the *meaning* of your question and finds the most relevant articles even when the words don’t match exactly. You can ask in **Nepali or English**. For example, *“पर्सामा निर्वाचनको तयारी कति अगाडि बढ्यो?”* returns relevant excerpts and links about election preparedness in Parsa. That makes it easier for ordinary citizens to find the news that matters to them, without having to know the right keywords or visit many sites.

### One place to see what’s in the news

Instead of opening many tabs and many websites, you can ask things like *“What’s going on in Nepal today?”* or *“What did the PM say about the budget?”* and get a concise answer plus a list of source headlines and links. The system is built so that **all responses are tied to real articles** in the database. You can use it as a starting point and then dig deeper into the sources you trust.

### You can see where a story came from (transparency)

The system is designed to record **who published a piece of content first** and who republished or adapted it. So the public can see “this story was first seen at outlet X at time Y” and “these outlets also carried it.” That makes the **origin of news visible**. You can judge whether you’re reading original reporting or something that was copied or syndicated — and credit goes where it is due.

### You can see how each outlet is performing (accountability)

We treat each **publisher (house)** as a distinct entity. The system is designed to **display reports per outlet**. That means the public can see, for each participating publisher:

- **What they published** — volume, topics, timeline.
- **Where they were first** — how often they broke a story versus republished.
- **How they framed the news** — in comparison to other outlets on the same topics (as we build narrative and bias analysis).
- **How their past claims held up** — once we have a verification loop (e.g. share of predictions that were later confirmed).

So the system doesn’t only help you find news — it helps you **see how each outlet is performing** and encourages a more **transparent and accountable** media landscape.

## Advantages of this system

- **Verifiable answers** — Every answer is tied to specific articles and source links. No black box: you can always check the original story.
- **One place, many sources** — You can query across multiple outlets at once instead of visiting each site separately. That saves time and helps you compare.
- **Nepali and English** — You can ask in the language you’re comfortable with. The system is built to serve Nepali-speaking users first.
- **Meaning-based search** — You don’t need to guess the right keywords. Ask in natural language; the system finds the most relevant content by meaning.
- **Transparency of origin** — You can see who published a story first and who republished it. That rewards original reporting and makes copying visible.
- **Accountability per outlet** — Reports per publisher (house) show what each outlet publishes, how they frame the news, and (over time) how often their claims are verified. That encourages responsible journalism.
- **Evidence-based discourse** — By grounding answers only in indexed news and by tracking claims over time, the system supports public debate based on what was actually said and what actually happened.
- **Level playing field** — Smaller or regional outlets can be part of the same index and the same reports. The public can discover and compare them alongside larger players.
- **No dependency on a single algorithm or company** — The project is designed so that search and Q&A are over *your* (the project’s) database of news, not over the whole web. That reduces the risk of hidden manipulation or opaque ranking.
- **Foundation for the future** — The knowledge-graph design (entities, provenance, verification) allows the system to evolve toward deeper transparency: who said what, whether it was true, and how outlets compare over time.

## Positive impact this project can bring

### For citizens and the general public

- **Better-informed decisions** — People can find and verify news in one place, in their language, and see the sources. That supports more informed participation in public life.
- **Stronger media literacy** — When origin and source are always visible, people learn to ask “where did this come from?” and “who said it first?” That habit helps them resist rumor and misinformation.
- **Easier access to diverse views** — Semantic search and multi-outlet indexing make it easier to discover coverage from different outlets on the same topic, so citizens can compare and form their own view.

### For democracy and public discourse

- **Evidence-based debate** — When answers and claims are tied to published articles and (over time) to verification outcomes, public discourse can rely more on evidence and less on unverified claims.
- **Accountability of power** — By tracking what was said and whether it came true, the system can help the public hold leaders and institutions to account. Outlets that report those claims are also visible, so their role is clearer.
- **Credit for original reporting** — Transparency of origin (“first seen at X”) rewards outlets that break stories and makes republishing visible. That can incentivize quality journalism.

### For the media and publishers

- **Incentive for accuracy and transparency** — When each outlet has a report (what they published, how they framed it, how their claims held up), there is a stronger incentive to be accurate and to distinguish clearly between fact and opinion.
- **Comparable, visible performance** — Outlets can see how they compare on originality, framing, and verification. The same data is available to the public, so the relationship between media and audience becomes more transparent.
- **Support for smaller and regional outlets** — They can be included in the same index and the same reporting framework, so the public can discover and trust them based on the same transparency criteria.

### For society in Nepal

- **A shared, verifiable record of news** — The system builds a searchable, attributable record of what was published, by whom, and when. That can serve researchers, educators, and future generations.
- **Less rumor, more sources** — By emphasizing “answer only from indexed news” and “always show sources,” the project encourages people to rely on published journalism and to check the original. That can reduce the spread of unverified information.
- **A step toward a more accountable and transparent news ecosystem** — NP News Hub is one building block: it doesn’t replace editorial judgment or regulation, but it gives the public and the media **tools** to see what was published, who said what, and how outlets are performing. Over time, that can support a healthier, more trustworthy news environment in Nepal.

## Why this system makes publishing more accountable and transparent

### Transparency: where did this story come from?

The system is designed to track **who published a piece of content first** and who republished or adapted it. The public can see “this story was first seen at outlet X at time Y” and “these outlets also carried it.” That makes **origin visible** and helps people judge whether they’re reading original reporting or something that was copied or syndicated.

### Accountability: how does each outlet behave?

Each **publisher (house)** is a distinct entity. The system is designed to:

- **Attribute every article to its publisher** — so we know which outlet published what.
- **Compare how different outlets cover the same topic** — tone, emphasis, what’s included or omitted — so the public can see differences in framing.
- **Track whether past claims and predictions came true** — so over time we can surface which outlets’ (or figures’) claims were verified and which were not.

That creates **accountability**: outlets know their coverage and their claims can be checked against the same system the public uses.

### Reports per publisher (house)

We will **display reports based on each house (publisher/outlet)**. For each participating outlet, the public can see:

- **What they published** — volume, topics, timeline.
- **Where they were first** — how often they broke or republished a story.
- **How they framed the news** — in comparison to other outlets on the same topics (as narrative and bias features are built).
- **How their past claims held up** — once the verification loop is in place (e.g. share of predictions that were later confirmed).

So the system doesn’t only help you find news — it helps you **see how each outlet is performing** and makes the whole landscape more **transparent and accountable**.

## What problems does it solve?

### 1. “I can’t find relevant articles when I search in Nepali.”

**What we do:** Semantic search finds the most **meaningfully similar** content. You get relevant excerpts and source links even when the exact words don’t match. Example: *“पर्सामा निर्वाचनको तयारी कति अगाडि बढ्यो?”* returns articles about election preparedness in Parsa.

### 2. “I want answers from the news, not from the whole internet.”

**What we do:** The Q&A flow uses **only** the indexed news database. Answers are grounded in real articles, with **source links** so you can verify. Example: *“What’s going on in Nepal today?”* gives you a short answer plus headlines and URLs from the database.

### 3. “I don’t know if this story is original or copied.”

**What we’re building:** Provenance and lineage (content hashing, discovery time) so you can see **who published first** and who else carried the same or similar content. That makes the flow of news **transparent**.

### 4. “I can’t tell how different outlets are covering the same topic.”

**What we’re building:** Story clustering and narrative/bias analysis so you can compare **how different outlets** cover the same event. Plus **reports per house** so you can see each outlet’s behaviour in one place.

### 5. “I want to know if an outlet’s or leader’s past claims came true.”

**What we’re building:** A verification loop that registers predictions, re-evaluates them later, and can feed into **publisher trust scores** and **house-level reports**. So the public can see which outlets and figures are more often verified over time.

## How does it work at a high level?

1. **Crawler** discovers article URLs; **Core** stores list and article content.
2. **Engine** chunks text, gets **embeddings** (e.g. via MPNet), and writes them into the database.
3. **Core** exposes **semantic search**: your question is matched against stored vectors; results come back with title, URL, snippet.
4. **Search** service powers the **/ask** endpoint: it calls Core, optionally uses an LLM to summarize, and always returns **sources** from the database.
5. **Web** is a simple chat interface: you type a question and get an answer plus source links.

So: **Crawler → Core (DB + embeddings) → Engine → Search (Q&A) → Web.**
